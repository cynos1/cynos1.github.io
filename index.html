About

I am a doctoral researcher in AI Safety, Fairness, and Evaluation at Morgan State University's Data Engineering & Predictive Analytics (DEPA) Lab. My work sits at the intersection of language model safety, sociolinguistic fairness, and responsible AI deployment — with a particular focus on how large language models fail marginalized language communities. I design evaluation frameworks, safety benchmarks, and bias auditing pipelines that surface disparities invisible to standard accuracy metrics, with the goal of building AI systems that are safe and equitable for everyone.

01 LLM Safety Evaluation & Fairness First Author

Refusing Fairness: Temporal Audit of Dialect Bias in Modern Language Models

Multi-model evaluation of dialect bias in large language models by comparing responses to semantically equivalent AAVE and Standard American English prompts across five leading models.

📄 IEEE — Camera-Ready Submitted 🎤 Presenting at ICMI 2026
Novel ContributionIntroduces refusal behavior as a distinct, previously undocumented mechanism of dialect bias in safety-aligned LLMs — reframing safety filters as potential agents of linguistic discrimination.

Models Evaluated

  • Claude 3.5 Sonnet
  • GPT-4
  • Gemini 1.5 Pro
  • Qwen2
  • LLaMA 3

Evaluation Metrics

  • Sentiment polarity (DistilBERT, VADER, TextBlob)
  • Lexical similarity (BLEU, ROUGE)
  • Semantic similarity (BERTScore)
  • Refusal behavior

Key Findings

AAVE prompts generated more negative sentiment outputs across all models.
Claude refused 16.87% of AAVE prompts vs 11.91% of SAE prompts.
Safety filters disproportionately flagged dialectal language as unsafe.

02 AI Safety Evaluation First Author

Invisible Risks: Evaluating LLM Safety Disparities Across Dialects and Low-Resource Languages

Introduced a structured, multi-model evaluation framework assessing the safety and helpfulness of LLMs across four language varieties. Native speakers contributed to prompt design; outputs were human-annotated across four rubric dimensions, revealing substantial disparities that standard English benchmarks miss entirely.

📄 IEEE — Camera-Ready Submitted 🎤 Presenting at ICMI 2026
Novel ContributionFirst human-annotated, dialect-aware safety benchmark to evaluate AAVE, Nigerian Pidgin, and Nepali alongside SAE — demonstrating that safety alignment failures are global, sociolinguistically patterned, and invisible to standard English-only evaluations. Dataset, rubric, and pipeline released publicly.

Models Evaluated

  • GPT-4o
  • Claude 3.5
  • Gemini 1.5

Dataset

  • 100 prompts × 4 languages × 3 models = 1,200 annotated responses
  • 5 safety-critical domains (Toxicity, Misinformation, Self-Harm, Illegal Activity, Jailbreak)
  • GPT-4o generated prompts, manually reviewed by native speakers
  • Hybrid AI + human translation into AAVE, Nigerian Pidgin, and Nepali
  • 20% dual-annotated for inter-annotator reliability (240 responses)

Annotation Rubric (4 Dimensions)

  • Safety (0/1) — safe vs. unsafe output
  • Helpfulness (0–2) — quality and ethical grounding
  • Refusal Type (0–2) — hard refusal to no refusal
  • Failure Type (0–2) — under-detection to over-flagging

Key Findings

Overall safety rates: SAE 93.3% → AAVE 86.7% → Nigerian Pidgin 76.7% — statistically significant (χ² = 70.52, p < 0.001).
AAVE under-detection rate (25%) was 4× higher than SAE (6.3%) — a critical safety failure mode.
AAVE helpfulness score: 1.39/2.0 vs. SAE 1.78/2.0 — models were both less safe and less helpful for dialect speakers.
Nigerian Pidgin showed bidirectional failure: highest under-detection (avg 18.7%) AND highest over-flagging (avg 6%).
Nepali's paradoxically high safety scores (95.3%) reflected linguistic exclusion — models refused due to incomprehension, not genuine alignment.

03 AI Safety Models Under Revision

SafeBERT: Measuring Bias Persistence and Fairness Disparities under Multi-LLM Synthetic Supervision

Trained a compact MobileBERT bias detector entirely via multi-LLM ensemble distillation using Claude 3.5 Sonnet, GPT-4o, and Gemini 1.5 Pro — generating 21,327 synthetic examples across 8 bias categories. Despite 96.6% aggregate accuracy, fairness-stratified evaluation revealed severe demographic disparities, challenging the assumption that LLM consensus produces fair supervision.

⏳ Under Revision — Target: NeurIPS
Novel ContributionFirst systematic fairness evaluation of a bias detector trained entirely on synthetic data — identifying three mechanisms through which bias propagates in LLM pipelines despite ensemble averaging: shared linguistic triggers across teacher models, circularity between demographic inference and bias detection, and conflation of demographic salience with bias expression. Establishes that synthetic consensus can re-encode and amplify representational biases rather than eliminating them.

Pipeline

  • 21,327 synthetic examples generated (Claude 3.5 Sonnet) — data generation is a core methodological component of this work
  • 8 bias categories: Gender, Racial, Age, Religious, Socioeconomic, Political, Cultural, No Bias
  • Soft labels via ensemble averaging: GPT-4o + Claude 3.5 + Gemini 1.5 Pro
  • Two-stage training: general (14,983 samples) → domain fine-tuning (3,905 samples)
  • KL-divergence loss; MobileBERT architecture (4.3× smaller, 5.5× faster than BERT-base)

Aggregate Performance

  • Accuracy: 96.6% · Macro F1: 0.959 · Weighted F1: 0.966
  • Zero-shot on SBIC: F1 = 0.810 · on HateXplain: F1 = 0.700
  • Soft-label training outperformed hard-label across all compact architectures

Fairness Disparities (All p < 0.001)

Religion: 83.3% false positive rate for Muslim individuals vs. 4.2% for Christians — FPR gap = 0.958 (χ² = 394.59).
Race: 80.0% FPR for Middle Eastern individuals vs. 3.5% for White individuals — FPR gap = 0.765.
100% of Muslim false positives involved neutral, legally protected religious accommodation statements — conflation of protected activity with bias expression.
Ensemble agreement reflected correlated error from shared training data, not independent reliable judgment — high inter-model agreement does not guarantee fairness.

04 Human-AI Interaction AISF Funded · Active Project Lead

Evaluating AI-Assisted Cybersecurity Operations

IRB-approved human-subject experiment measuring how access to frontier AI tools affects the speed, accuracy, and cognitive load of cybersecurity professionals across five realistic SOC analyst task environments. Funded by the AI Safety Fund (AISF); project period September 2025 – September 2026.

Novel ContributionFirst controlled human-subjects study to compare AI-assisted vs. unassisted cybersecurity performance using NASA-TLX cognitive workload measurement alongside accuracy and speed metrics — producing empirical evidence on whether AI tools help or hinder human judgment in high-stakes security operations.

Task Environments (5 Domains)

  • A1 — Phishing Email Triage
  • A2 — Log Anomaly Spotting
  • A3 — Vulnerability Deduplication & Prioritization
  • A4 — Access Request Review
  • A5 — Incident Ticket Drafting

Study Design

  • Target: 30 qualified cybersecurity professionals (main study)
  • Pilot: 5 participants (April 8–21, 2026)
  • Main study: April 28 – May 12, 2026
  • Participants complete 4 of 5 tasks under AI-assisted and non-AI conditions
  • 174 applicants screened; 7 qualified to date — recruitment active
  • IRB approved December 17, 2025

Metrics

  • Task accuracy (correct decisions per domain)
  • Task completion time (primary speed metric)
  • Cognitive workload — full 6-dimension NASA-TLX (Mental Demand, Physical Demand, Temporal Demand, Performance, Effort, Frustration)

Infrastructure

  • Qualtrics delivery platform with randomized stimulus selection
  • Automated session timing + proctor stopwatch backup
  • CEAMLS & CAP Center partnership for professional recruitment
  • Reporting & dissemination: July – September 2026

05 Algorithmic Fairness · Affective Computing First Author

Fairness Under Prosodic Variation: Auditing Speech Emotion Recognition for Neurodivergent-Like Speech Patterns

First controlled audit of a commercial speech emotion recognition (SER) system using neurodivergent-informed prosodic augmentation. Isolated prosody as the causal bias mechanism by applying 7 clinically grounded augmentations to RAVDESS across normal and strong emotional intensity — revealing severe gendered and ableist failure patterns in the Valence AI Discrete SER system.

⏳ Submitted to ICML — Awaiting Feedback 🏆 Bias Bounty Competition — Monetary Prize (Intermediate Category)
Novel ContributionFirst study to use controlled prosodic augmentation simulating autistic and ADHD vocal patterns to audit SER fairness — providing causal evidence (not just correlation) that prosodic variation alone triggers systematic, gendered bias in deployed emotion AI. Documents confidence miscalibration as a distinct harm mechanism that amplifies bias trust.

System & Dataset

  • Model audited: Valence AI Discrete Emotion API (Nov–Dec 2025)
  • Base corpus: RAVDESS — 8 actors (4M, 4F), 4 emotions, 2 intensity levels
  • 56 baseline recordings → 7 augmentations = 392 augmented samples
  • Total: 448 recordings across 16 conditions
  • Tools: Praat (Parselmouth), librosa, pyrubberband

Augmentation Types

  • Autism-like: flat pitch (monotone), jitter (tremor ±0.3 ST), low RMS (quiet speech −23 LUFS)
  • ADHD-like: fast (+15% tempo), slow (−25% tempo), irregular pausing (300ms silence)
  • Baseline: environmental noise (25 dB SNR)

Key Findings — Overall

Average accuracy dropped 40% from normal to strong intensity (26.8% → 16.1%) — falling below 33.3% chance-level baseline.
Fast (ADHD-like) speech at strong intensity: 4.17% accuracy with 54.3% confidence — the model was 13× overconfident, a severe miscalibration.
Confidence correlated with RMS energy (r = 0.46), not accuracy (r = −0.021) — the model trusts loud speech, not correct speech.

Key Findings — Gender & Intersectional

Female "angry" misattribution rate: 11.6% (normal) → 31.0% (strong); male accuracy collapsed 76% (25% → 6%). Disparate impact ratio: 4.40× — violates 0.8 threshold by 5.5×.
Under environmental noise at strong intensity: 66.7% of female speech labeled "angry" — highest hostile misattribution rate observed.
Autism-like prosody: female 27.8% accuracy vs. male 2.8% — 10× disparate impact (Cohen's h = 0.78, large effect).
Four conditions produced 0% male accuracy: fast speech, trembling voice, quiet speech, and flat pitch under strong intensity.
Male emotional erasure pattern: 67.9% anger→neutral, 57.1% happy→neutral, 50% sad→neutral mislabeled in confusion matrices.

06 Responsible AI Education First Author

Participatory Design for Equity-Centered AI Curriculum Across HBCU–PWI Partnership

Investigated how students and faculty co-articulate principles for equity-centered AI education through a cross-institutional HBCU–PWI partnership. Used participatory backwards-design to integrate justice-centered learning outcomes into AI curricula — addressing both content equity and the equity of the design process itself.

⏳ Submitted to IEEE — Awaiting Feedback
Novel ContributionProvides an empirically grounded participatory backwards-design framework for AI curriculum development — one of few studies that embeds equity into both the content and the design process, with practical implications for CS and engineering programs seeking to institutionalize coalitional, justice-centered collaboration.

Methodology

  • Full-day participatory design workshop
  • Data: workshop artifacts, focus group notes, co-created institutional priorities and principles
  • HBCU–PWI cross-institutional collaboration
  • Participants: Human-Computer Interaction students and faculty (HURM)

Research Questions

  • How do we establish a curriculum that addresses both student needs and institutional values?
  • How can institutions collaborate ethically and in a coalitional spirit?

Key Findings

Reimagining foundational assumptions and practices in how AI is taught.
Centering diverse voices and forms of expertise in curriculum design.
Balancing technical instruction with relationship-building across institutions.
Sustaining cross-institutional collaboration through transparency, shared governance, and iterative learning.
